Barely a week goes by without the tech media reporting on a new company offering "no-code" software getting funded, being acquired, or even going public. No-code and it's slightly more techy cousin, low-code, are jargon for software that enables workers in a business to create software without (much) coding ability.

Tools in the no-code category do a wide variety of different things - there are website builders (Squarespace), databases (Airtable), workflow makers which connect disparate software (Zapier), data visualization tools (Tableau) and many others. Each of these areas actually has a number of contenders, with a slightly different emphasis or positioning. And there are almost as many "low-code" tools which are generally aimed at larger enterprises.

So why this class of software is having a "moment" right now? In the past, businesses would encounter a need for software and would have to make a "build vs. buy" decision. In other words, would off-the-shelf software meet the business requirement or would building a custom tool be more beneficial in the long run? There were (and are) pluses and minuses to each approach.

What has changed in the contemporary business environment is the faster pace of change, the  massive (and ever growing) quantity of data that need to be anaylzed and operated on, and the reduced cause of (cloud) computing power. There is usually not the time to run a long "process" to make a build-buy decision, nor are the developers needed to build a traditional application cheap or easy to find. So companies are now induced to blend buying things with building them. You might, for instance, purchase the finance team subscriptions to Tableau with which they are charged with creating/updating executive dashboards. Or, to give another example, someone in Marketing might use Zapier to connect data that comes in via social media or via online surveys to the company's CRM of choice.

Will no-code and low-code tools supercede traditional software development? That is the hype around many of the superstar companies in the sector, but the bet here is that at scale, it will still make more sense for companies to build many tools programmatically. The learning curve for the no-code tools is less steep than learning SQL or Javascript, but you end up with siloed knowledge about a number of proprietary tools. Meanwhile, the investment in learning a generalizable, standards-driven tool like SQL will continue to pay benefits over the course of many projects.
